Experience

Onset Computer Corp.

Software Engineering Intern

Onset's goal is to provide a means of measuring, storing, and managing environmental data through the use of loggers and monitors. These devices are used to collect information about the surrounding environment including but not limited to: water temperature and salinity, wind speeds, soil composition and humidity. They are often used in renewable energy systems or in agriculture and oceanic research. The loggers drastically simplify the data collection process by sending data directly to a data warehouse that Onset manages. Onset manufactures the montiors, loggers, and other devices needed, as well as writes the supporting software code.

I held a Software Engineering internship at Onset during the summer of 2021, working to enhance their software testing capabilities. Onset hosts several data warehouses; the largest one reserved for customers. In order to test their software and data load maximums, one of these such warehouses was dedicated to testing. Testing is an extremely important part of software engineering, especially when serving a large number of customers. I was part of a small team which was tasked with implementing a number of different programs which would test the load that the redshift servers were able to operate under. More specifically, I used SQL queries to dynamically gather data from the customer database on the number and type of each device that was active. I was then tasked to emulate each of these devices, and provide user commands so that the number and type of devices could be changed for different testing needs.

This software was implemented in Java, one of the languages I am most familiar with. As the only summer intern, and under the restrictions of Covid-19, I spent time familiarizing myself with agile software development and setting up a work laptop which could access and pull data from Onset's data warehouses.

St. Andrew's School

Camp Director and Lead

A private school in Barrington, Rhode Island, St. Andrew's School hosts summer programs for kids aged 8-15. In addition to athletic and artistic activities, these programs also include engineering, robotics, and 3D design components, which I had been a mentor for; after a few years, I was overseeing the entire program.

One of the computer science and circuitry curriculums I helped to implement involved using Arduinos and breadboards. Though the introduction to coding was very difficult for most students of such a young age, we had groups that created a version of hangman using the LCD screen, or a Mario Kart timer using LED's. We would also spend time with more mechanical and simpler projects, such as egg drops and cars, aluminum foil boats, spaghetti towers, and paper airplane competitions!

For the robotics section of the camp, we would work with LEGO Mindstorms or Tetrix robots. The campers would start building from instructions, to learn how the different parts and pieces interact. From there, we would design different events for them to build robots to compete in. These events included sumo-bots, an obstacle course, drag racing, and tug-of-war. Oftentimes the groups would take inspiration from the BattleBots television series, adding claws or plows to their robots!

The design camp was often my favorite week of camp to lead. I would show the group the basics of 3D printing and how printers operate, giving them a live demo with my home-built 3D printer! We would research the extent to which 3D printers could be used (they are used to print parts for Spacecraft!) before sending them off to use a simple CAD software to design their own creations! Whether it was armies of tiny action figures, dream sports cars, or huge mansions, we would use the 3D printers in the classroom to print as many of their creations as possible before the end of the camp.

Computer Vision

Chess Board Recognition

Chess has always fascinated me. The number of possible positions is near uncountable and has even been given a name - Shannon's number. After taking a computer vision course, I knew exactly how to apply my newfound knowledge; and perhaps win a few chess games by the end.

And so, I designed a program which uses a convolutional neural network to convert a given image into a digital board position. The network trained on an online dataset of top-down chess boards. Each image was divided up into 64 different inputs, found by using a line detection algorithm to split the board up into it's squared in order to run the model on a single piece at a time. The outputs were then stitched back together in order to create a final board output.

Though I did not complete an AI that played well enough to present a challenge to Stockfish, I used an online library which I could pass the board state into to produce the best move given the input position. I knew a few victories were in my future!

Prusa i3

My home-built 3D printer

I used to run my hands through my three LEGO bins, hunting for the piece I needed to complete my creation. In my frustration I dreamed of being able to produce just the piece I needed, in the color I wanted, in the time that I felt I didn't want to waste. When I heard about 3D printers, I was mystified at the prospect that my dream might finally come true.

When I was finally given the chance in high school, I jumped. Along with two of my friends, and the help of a kind and wonderful mentor, I began learning about 3D printing and the engineering process. We worked as a team to complete a 3D printer for our mentor, built entirely from scratch. Once satisfied with our handiwork, we used the now functional printer to fashion parts for our own 3D printers.

After a few months of "brute force and ignorance", as our mentor called it, each of us had finally completed our own working Prusa i3 printer from scratch! Using my SolidWorks knowledge, I was not only able to print the LEGO pieces I desired, but also design my own LEGO pieces that no one had ever seen before!
